Water levels fully recovered. After the severe 2023-24 drought, Gatun and Alajuela reservoirs are at their highest levels ever recorded for March. Alajuela is near 99% capacity, Gatun above 90%, allowing maximum Neopanamax draft of 50.0 feet. Daily transits have returned to 38-40 vessels — back to pre-drought norms.

Booking system overhauled. The new LoTSA 2.0 program shifted from 12-month to 6-month horizons, with the restriction on one Neopanamax booking per date eliminated as of January 2026. LNG carriers received reinstated priority booking rules.

Geopolitical wildcard. A Panamanian court transferred the Balboa and Cristobal ports from CK Hutchison to APM Terminals (Maersk), ending a 27-year concession. China retaliated: since March 8, nearly 70 Panama-flagged vessels have been detained at Chinese ports, 39% Japanese-owned. COSCO suspended Balboa operations.

Route shifts persist. Many Asia-to-US East Coast services now route via Suez (adding 10-14 days) to avoid Panama congestion risk. The canal handles ~5% of global seaborne trade. ACP expects a temporary traffic bump from tariff front-loading on Chinese goods.

Volume under pressure. LA processed 9.4M TEUs in 11 months (+19% YoY), Long Beach 8.8M TEUs (+20.2%), NY/NJ 7.2M TEUs. But the NRF projects H1 2026 imports at 12.27M TEUs across major ports — a 2% decline vs H1 2025. Moody's has flagged weakening outlook as tariffs bite.

Tariff headwinds real. An average effective tariff rate of ~17% is weighing on flows. Rather than sustained congestion, ports are seeing episodic "volume bunching" as importers game tariff timing. Import processing averages 3.33 days (up 8.7% from mid-2025 lows).

Gulf Coast expansion accelerating. Port of Mobile completed deepening to 50 feet — now the deepest on the Gulf. Galveston advancing a $106M cargo complex upgrade (new 46-foot berth by H2 2026). Houston's Care Terminal commissioned two new 120-tonne cranes in March. LNG infrastructure continues expanding at Golden Pass and Plaquemines.

Cross-Cutting Insights

  • Supply chain fragility persists: The Panama Canal is physically recovered but geopolitically destabilized. China's detention of Panama-flagged vessels creates a new chokepoint risk that intersects with tariff-driven trade reshuffling.
  • Overcapacity will define 2026: A 36% fleet capacity surge (2023-2027) meets softening demand (-2% US imports) and potential Red Sea normalization releasing 1.75M TEU. Container rates have room to fall further.
  • Gulf Coast is the growth story: While West Coast ports face volume pressure, Gulf ports are deepening channels, adding cranes, and riding LNG export growth. Mobile's 50-foot channel and Houston's terminal upgrades position them for larger vessels.
  • Tariff timing drives volatility: The 17% average tariff rate is creating episodic volume bunching rather than steady flows, making port operations and freight rate forecasting unusually unpredictable.
  • Route diversification is structural: The shift of Asia-USEC services from Panama to Suez isn't temporary — carriers have rebuilt networks around it, and the China-Panama dispute reinforces the trend.
