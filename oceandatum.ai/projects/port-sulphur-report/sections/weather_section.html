Executive Summary

Port Sulphur Terminal operates at Mississippi River Mile 48.6 AHP within a remarkably consistent annual hydrologic cycle, shaped by three distinct seasonal patterns. The location experiences extreme variations in river discharge (131,000 to 650,000+ CFS), water velocity (1.2 to 5.0+ fps), and compound flooding risks from storm surge, river stage, and precipitation. However, post-Katrina levee improvements provide 100-year level protection, and the terminal's deep-water access creates strategic advantages during extreme low-water events.

Critical operational finding: The prime construction window (July-September) overlaps with peak hurricane season, requiring flexible scheduling and enhanced monitoring protocols. Winter low-water periods (November-February) offer ideal conditions for underwater work and maintenance, while spring high-water events (March-June) present the most challenging conditions with extreme current velocities.

Historical Flood Events & Infrastructure Resilience

Port Sulphur has experienced both catastrophic storm surge events and extreme river flooding, providing a comprehensive dataset for design envelope development. The 2005 Hurricane Katrina event with ~22 feet of inundation at Port Sulphur led to significant federal levee improvements under the HSDRRS (Hurricane and Storm Damage Risk Reduction System), which have been successfully validated during subsequent hurricanes Isaac (2012) and Ida (2021).

Compound Flooding Scenarios

Port Sulphur faces unique compound flooding risks from the simultaneous interaction of multiple water sources. A tropical system can produce:

  • Storm surge from Gulf of Mexico: 8-22+ feet depending on storm characteristics
  • Mississippi River stage elevation: Can coincide with spring high water (March-June)
  • Tropical rainfall: 5-15 inches over multi-day periods
  • Timing interactions: When these factors align, regional flooding extends beyond immediate storm areas

The 2019 extended spring flood event (>6 months sustained high water) coinciding with active hurricane season provided valuable data on infrastructure performance under compound stress scenarios. Federal levee protection has proven effective, but operational planning must account for these multi-factor risk periods.

Hurricane Season Risk Management

Port Sulphur lies in the primary threat zone for Gulf of Mexico tropical systems, with hurricane season running June 1 - November 30 and peak activity during August-October. This creates a critical operational planning challenge: the prime construction window (July-September) overlaps directly with elevated hurricane risk.

Risk mitigation strategies:

  • 5-day advance forecasts: National Hurricane Center provides sufficient warning for preparation
  • Flexible scheduling: Construction projects must accommodate weather-related pauses
  • Enhanced monitoring: Peak season (August-October) requires continuous weather surveillance
  • Infrastructure protection: Federal HSDRRS levee system provides 100-year level protection
  • Historical validation: Post-Katrina improvements successfully tested during Isaac (2012) and Ida (2021)

Detailed Weather Patterns

Precipitation (62-65 inches annually)

Unevenly distributed throughout the year with distinct seasonal patterns:

  • Summer (June-September): Intense convective activity with several inches possible in short time periods, peak intensity during late afternoon/evening hours
  • Winter (December-February): Longer-duration, lower-intensity systems with minimal but persistent impact
  • Tropical events: 5-15 inches over multi-day periods, often coinciding with elevated river stages

Wind Conditions

Summer: South winds 5-10 knots typical, 1-2 foot seas in coastal approaches, thunderstorm activity creates variable conditions

Winter: More variable wind directions; cold fronts bring sustained winds 30-40 mph with higher gusts for 24-48 hour durations, challenging vessel operations and potentially requiring temporary suspension of cargo handling

Hurricane season: June 1 - November 30, most significant weather-related operational risk with 5-day advance forecasts available

Fog Conditions (15-25 days annually)

  • Peak period: December - March (winter season)
  • Formation mechanism: Radiational cooling combined with river surface moisture
  • Typical duration: Early morning through mid-morning; occasionally all-day events
  • Visibility threshold: Dense fog <0.25 miles requires vessel movement suspension
  • Economic impact: Navigation restrictions and cargo handling limitations accumulate over fog season

  • Regional coordination: Fog reporting and visibility monitoring network

Competitive Advantages & Strategic Positioning

Deep-Water Access

The terminal's deep-water capability provides significant competitive advantages during extreme low-water periods when shallow terminals face draft restrictions. The 2023 extreme low water event (131,000 CFS) demonstrated this strategic positioning, creating revenue opportunities while competitors experienced operational constraints.

Federal Levee Protection

Location within the HSDRRS (Hurricane and Storm Damage Risk Reduction System) provides 100-year level of risk reduction (1% annual exceedance probability). This protection has been validated through major storms including Hurricane Isaac (2012) and Hurricane Ida (2021), with visible water lines on levees confirming system effectiveness.

Year-Round Operational Capability

Unlike many terminals that face significant seasonal constraints, Port Sulphur maintains operational capability across the full range of river conditions through seasonal optimization:

  • Spring high water (Mar-Jun): Maximum draft capability despite challenging currents
  • Summer/fall transition (Jul-Oct): Optimal operations and construction windows
  • Winter low water (Nov-Feb): Competitive advantage for deep-water access, ideal for maintenance

Notable Historical Events - Design Envelope

Hurricane Katrina (August 2005)

  • Storm surge: 15-19 feet (Plaquemines Parish regional); ~22 feet inundation at Port Sulphur site
  • Impact: Catastrophic damage to sulfur facility structures; ~80% housing units affected in parish
  • Data quality: Multiple high-water mark datasets from FEMA WWL maps and USGS surveys
  • Legacy: Led to comprehensive federal levee improvements under HSDRRS program
  • Sources: NOAA/NHC TCR (updated Jan 2023), FEMA Wind-Water Line Data (June 2006), USGS SIR 2009-5230

2018 Spring Flood

  • Peak discharge: ~650,000 CFS (record-approaching)
  • Significance: Establishes design envelope for extreme peak flow events
  • Duration: Extended high-water period (March-June typical pattern)
  • Operational impact: Demonstrated structural loading under extreme conditions

2019 Extended Flood

  • Duration: >6 months sustained high water (exceptional)
  • Significance: Most extended high-water event in monitoring record
  • Value: Valuable dataset on infrastructure performance under prolonged extreme conditions
  • Construction impact: Demonstrated need for flexible scheduling during spring/early summer

Hurricane Isaac (2012)

  • Significance: First major test of post-Katrina levee improvements
  • Observations: "Port Sulphur is good; Buras is good; Venice is good"
  • Evidence: Visible water lines on levees showed protection systems held during significant surge
  • Validation: No major damage reported, confirming infrastructure effectiveness

Hurricane Ida (2021)

  • Impact: Significant infrastructure resilience test
  • Storm surge: ~12 feet (estimated regional surge)
  • Outcome: Post-Katrina levee systems validated again
  • Long-term effects: Debris from event remains visible years later, demonstrating hurricane impacts

2023 Extreme Low Water

  • Discharge: 131,000 CFS (extreme low)
  • Impact: Industry-wide draft restrictions at shallow terminals
  • Strategic advantage: Port Sulphur's deep-water access maintained full operations
  • Market opportunity: Revenue generation while competitors faced operational constraints

Long-term Climate Considerations

Future planning for Port Sulphur Terminal must account for evolving climate factors:

  • Sea level rise: Expected gradual increase in storm surge potential and compound flooding risk
  • Subsidence and settlement: Ongoing monitoring of structure settlement and levee elevation maintenance
  • Storm intensity/frequency: Potential increases in hurricane severity or frequency requiring design adaptability
  • River hydrology changes: Watershed-scale changes in runoff patterns may affect seasonal discharge timing

The HSDRRS federal levee system incorporates these factors in design standards, with regular review and update cycles to maintain 100-year level of protection as climate conditions evolve.
