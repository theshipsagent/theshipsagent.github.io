Engineering Overview

Port Sulphur Terminal (River Mile 39) sits entirely within the federally-protected Port Sulphur Revetment reach (RM 41.1-34.3), creating a complex engineering environment where Mississippi River dynamics intersect with comprehensive bank protection systems. The site's engineering requirements are defined by extreme current velocity variations (1.0-4.0 ft/sec), articulated concrete mat revetment systems requiring specialized penetration procedures, and seasonal operational patterns that create distinct construction windows.

Current velocity patterns drive structural design requirements: spring high water (March-June) generates maximum loading at 2.5-4.0 ft/sec, summer/fall normal periods (July-October) provide optimal construction conditions at 1.5-2.5 ft/sec, and winter low water (November-February) enables underwater work at 1.0-1.5 ft/sec. All infrastructure must accommodate this operational envelope while complying with USACE June 1999 standard procedures for revetment penetrations.

Critical Revetment Engineering Standards

USACE June 1999 Standard Drawing

"Repair Procedures when Penetrating Revetments with Piles, Caissons, and/or Pile Clusters"

Fundamental Principle

No penetration of concrete mat or trench-fill systems without approved repair procedures that maintain or enhance original protective function. All penetrations must implement engineered solutions under USACE engineering oversight.

Technical Requirements
  • Collar and Seal Systems: Engineered systems spanning full thickness of protective mat
  • Toe Protection: Additional provisions required at base of revetment
  • Long-term Monitoring: Continuous performance assessment of penetration integrity
  • Mat Stress Management: Construction sequencing to minimize stress on articulated mats
  • Engineering Oversight: All penetration procedures performed under qualified engineering supervision
Construction Constraints
  • Treat revetment systems as critical infrastructure, not work platforms
  • Minimize heavy construction traffic on mats to preserve engineered flexibility
  • Avoid lateral loading from equipment operations
  • Implement construction sequencing that minimizes mat stress
  • Approved penetration procedures required for all mat interactions

Anchoring Restrictions - 33 CFR 207.200

Federal regulations prohibit anchoring over revetted banks without authorization

Regulatory Framework

Coast Pilot 5 for the Lower Mississippi River explicitly notes anchoring restrictions over revetted banks. This regulation recognizes that uncontrolled anchoring can damage protective systems while creating navigation hazards.

Compliance Requirements
  • Vessel Positioning: Must use designated anchorage areas or approved mooring systems
  • Construction Activities: Specialized positioning plans required for marine construction
  • Operational Mooring: Permanent mooring systems must integrate with revetment protection
  • Authorization Process: USACE approval required for any anchoring over revetments

Critical Engineering Considerations

Bank Stability History

Multiple revetment systems (Point Michel, Port Sulphur, Bohemia) indicate historically unstable banks requiring comprehensive protection. Foundation design must account for underlying bank instability and integrate with existing federal infrastructure.

Current Velocity Extremes

Design envelope of 1.0-4.0 ft/sec (400% variation) requires structural systems capable of accommodating both extreme loading and variable operational conditions. Heavy-duty mooring, fender, and foundation systems mandatory.

Scour Protection Criticality

Extensive hardening and high current velocities make scour protection a critical design element. Protection systems must function across full velocity range and integrate with revetment penetration procedures.

Regulatory Integration Complexity

Development within federal Mississippi River & Tributaries project triggers Section 408, Section 10, and Section 404 requirements. Coordination with USACE New Orleans District required throughout design and construction phases.
