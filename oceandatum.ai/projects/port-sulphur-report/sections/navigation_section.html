Underwater Depth Zones

Near-Shore Transition Zone 0 to -20 ft

Gradual slope typical of Mississippi River banks. Limited draft access in immediate shore zone. May require approach channel dredging for large vessels depending on operational requirements.

Shallow Draft Operations
Primary Docking Zone -20 to -60 ft

Existing dock structure vicinity supporting McMoran operations. Suitable for medium-draft vessels. Current capability: 52-foot draft at low river stage. Adequate depths for additional berth development.

Medium Draft Vessels Supported
Deep Navigation Channel -60 to -120 ft

Main Mississippi River navigation channel maintained by USACE to -45 ft minimum. Natural depths of 60-120 feet exceed federal requirements and support Panamax and Post-Panamax vessels. Deep water proximity provides exceptional operational flexibility and competitive advantage.

Panamax & Post-Panamax Capable

Seasonal River Conditions

Spring High Water (March-June): Discharge exceeds 375,000 cfs with current velocities of 2.5-5.0 fps. Maximum draft access with deepest available depths, but substantial operational challenges from high current stress. Requires enhanced mooring and additional tug assistance.

Summer/Fall Normal (July-October): Discharge 222,000-379,000 cfs with velocities 1.5-2.5 fps. Optimal navigation scenarios with balance of adequate depth and manageable current forces. Ideal conditions for construction activities and routine operations.

Winter Low Water (November-February): Discharge 131,000-222,000 cfs with velocities 1.0-1.5 fps. Potential draft restrictions at other terminals during extreme low water events, but Port Sulphur's exceptional depths (60-120 ft) provide competitive advantage when shallow-draft terminals face restrictions.

Navigation Aids & Infrastructure

  • Port Sulphur Anchorage (RM 37.5-39.7) - Designated anchorage eliminates restrictions, facility within anchorage zone
  • Home Place Lights (RM 37.0-37.6) - Downstream reference points for navigation

Strategic Competitive Advantages

  • Deeper than most Mississippi River terminals - Many terminals limited to 40-45 feet; Port Sulphur maintains 60-120 feet
  • Operations during low-water periods - Maintains full operations when competitors face draft restrictions
  • Direct federal channel access - No private dredging costs, shared federal maintenance burden
  • Shorter transit to Gulf - 39 miles versus 60+ miles for upriver terminals
  • Within designated anchorage - Pre-approved staging eliminates anchoring restrictions
  • Two-way vessel traffic capable - Adequate channel width for simultaneous large vessel operations
