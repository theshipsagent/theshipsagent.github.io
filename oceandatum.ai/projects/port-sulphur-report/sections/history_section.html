Post-Civil War to Early Industrialization

1870 - 1932
‚ñº
Post-Reconstruction Plaquemines Parish shifted to commercial fishing, oyster harvesting, and citrus farming. The property remained undeveloped marsh until sulfur deposits were identified in the early 1900s. The Frasch Process (patented 1894) enabled sulfur extraction from salt domes using superheated water. Geological surveys identified sulfur deposits at Lake Grande √âcaille. In 1912, Freeport Sulphur Company was founded, establishing the city of Freeport, Texas near its sulfur mines.

Facility Construction and Initial Operations

1932 - 1939
‚ñº
In 1932, Freeport Sulphur Company acquired sulfur mining rights for Lake Grande √âcaille (10 miles west of Port Sulphur). Facility construction began in 1932-1933 to support logistics, refining, and shipping operations. The 24' x 433' concrete dock was completed in 1933, rated for 10,000 tons with 52' draft access. Port Sulphur facility began operations in 1933, processing sulfur from Grande √âcaille via the Frasch Process. The company town of Port Sulphur grew around the site with worker housing and supporting infrastructure.

Peak Operational Period

1940 - 1978
‚ñº
From 1933-1978, Grande √âcaille served as the primary sulfur mine with Port Sulphur as the logistics and shipping hub. The facility supported multiple Frasch Process sulfur mines throughout Louisiana, including offshore Main Pass Block 299 (50 miles offshore, 300' water depth). Wartime production peaked during WWII, earning an Army-Navy "E" Award in 1944 for defense contributions. Expansions in the 1950s added liquid sulfur shipment capabilities (first barge load shipped in 1955). By the 1960s-1970s, the facility operated at peak capacity with extensive "vatting" operations creating acres of dry sulfur storage blocks.

Economic Decline and Closure

1979 - 2005
‚ñº
Primary sulfur mining operations ceased at Grande √âcaille mine in 1978 after 45 years. Economic decline began in the 1980s due to market competition from recovered sulfur (oil/gas refining byproducts). In 1981, McMoRan Oil & Gas merged with Freeport Minerals to create Freeport-McMoRan Inc. (FTX). Reduced operations continued through the 1990s at diminished capacity. By the early 2000s, all sulfur operations ceased. Environmental concerns emerged including acid generation, heavy metals mobilization, and groundwater impacts from 72 years of operations. Hurricane Katrina (August 29, 2005) delivered catastrophic damage with 22' storm surge and 75 kt winds, destroying remaining infrastructure and marking the definitive end of Freeport operations.

Post-Katrina Remediation and Current Status

2005 - Present
‚ñº
Post-Katrina site clearance operations began with FEMA assistance, including $1.5M funding for water treatment infrastructure recovery in 2007. In 2007, FCX acquired Phelps Dodge, becoming the world's largest publicly traded copper producer. Environmental assessments were conducted in 2009 under Louisiana DEQ's Risk Evaluation/Corrective Action Program (RECAP) addressing sulfuric acid and heavy metals contamination. Freeport-McMoRan conducted comprehensive remediation from 2020-2024 involving lime neutralization, soil amendments, and groundwater treatment. In 2024, remediation was completed and the site received verified "ready for reuse" status from LDEQ. EPA ECHO database confirms no ongoing enforcement actions. As of September 2025, the 106.99-acre industrial waterfront property is listed for $14.7M, emphasizing deep-water access for potential dock expansion.
