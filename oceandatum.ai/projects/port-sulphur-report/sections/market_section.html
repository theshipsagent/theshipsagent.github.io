Mississippi River System Traffic Composition

The Mississippi River System's cargo mix is dominated by grain exports (60%), petroleum products (17.5%), and coal (12.5%), with the remaining 10% comprising steel, aggregates, and chemicals. This downbound export flow originates from upper river points (St. Louis, Mid-Mississippi) and moves to Gulf terminals near Mile Marker 0, bypassing most intermediate ports.

Market Insight

80-90% of Mississippi River traffic is captive, locked into integrated supply chains by major shippers like Cargill and ADM. Large tows (35-45 barges) minimize intermediate handling due to economies of scale and contractual obligations. Only 10-20% involves local ashore handling at lower river ports (MM 0-95).

Barge Transportation Economics

Barge rates from St. Louis to Gulf ports average $17.94 per ton under normal conditions (January 2025), but low-water events create extreme volatility. During the 2023 drought crisis, rates peaked at $52.91 per ton (+195% vs. baseline), driven by draft restrictions reducing barge capacity by 27% and forcing daylight-only tows.

MM39-Specific Market Opportunity

Mile Marker 39 (Port Sulphur Terminal) has access to 5-10 million tons annually of non-captive industrial cargo, but current capture rate remains at 1-2%. Primary commodities include cement imports, metals, and industrial materials. The critical constraint is rail connectivity via the New Orleans & Gulf Coast Railway (NOGC).

Infrastructure & Rail Connectivity

The New Orleans & Gulf Coast Railway (NOGC) is a 32-mile short line serving the Westbank, with interchange to Union Pacific at Westwego. Plaquemines Port has advanced rail-spur concepts to connect future terminals to NOGC, critical for improving the current 1-2% capture rate to higher levels.
