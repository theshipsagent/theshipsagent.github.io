Executive Summary

Critical due diligence assessment for Port Sulphur Terminal acquisition reveals significant environmental liabilities, extreme flood vulnerabilities, and substantial financial exposure. The site's 45+ years of sulfur processing operations, combined with its below-sea-level location and history of catastrophic flooding (22 feet during Hurricane Katrina in 2005), create complex challenges requiring comprehensive third-party assessments and specialized legal counsel.

Key Recommendations

1

Due Diligence Protocol

Engage qualified environmental consultants immediately for Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (ESA). This initial screening is mandatory before proceeding with transaction discussions and will determine the need for Phase II investigations.

2

Regulatory Compliance Review

Verify current RCRA generator status, EPA oversight requirements, and all ongoing environmental obligations. Non-NPL Superfund site status creates complex compliance requirements that must be fully understood before acquisition.

3

Flood Risk & Insurance Analysis

Commission comprehensive flood risk assessment given site's below-sea-level location, history of 22-foot flooding during Katrina, and Plaquemines Parish's world-leading sea level rise rates. Insurance feasibility and costs are critical to project viability.

4

Financial Reserves & Contingencies

Budget $10-30 million for potential environmental remediation based on site's contamination history and operational legacy. Establish separate reserves for ongoing monitoring, maintenance, and compliance obligations that extend beyond remediation.

5

Specialized Legal Counsel

Retain environmental attorneys with Superfund site experience to structure transaction in a manner that appropriately allocates liability, ensures regulatory compliance, and protects purchaser from unforeseen environmental obligations. Transaction structure is critical to managing long-term risk.

6

Third-Party Technical Reports

Secure independent engineering assessments for bulkhead/wharf conditions, geotechnical investigations for subsurface stability, and sediment testing if dredging is contemplated. These reports inform both development feasibility and cost modeling.

7

Transaction Structuring Options

Explore seller financing arrangements, federal/state environmental incentives, brownfield tax credits, and innovative liability allocation mechanisms. Complex environmental sites often benefit from creative deal structures that balance risk and opportunity.
