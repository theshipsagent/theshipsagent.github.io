Seasonal River Conditions

Spring High Water (March-June): Discharge exceeds 375,000 cfs with current velocities of 2.5-5.0 fps. Maximum draft access with deepest available depths, but substantial operational challenges from high current stress. Requires enhanced mooring and additional tug assistance.

Summer/Fall Normal (July-October): Discharge 222,000-379,000 cfs with velocities 1.5-2.5 fps. Optimal navigation scenarios with balance of adequate depth and manageable current forces. Ideal conditions for construction activities and routine operations.

Winter Low Water (November-February): Discharge 131,000-222,000 cfs with velocities 1.0-1.5 fps. 1981: Corporate Merger

Historical Periods

Prehistoric and Indigenous Period

1000 BCE - 1500 CE
▼
Archaeological evidence indicates human occupation dating to at least 1000 BCE, with shell middens and ceramic artifacts from the Marksville and Coles Creek cultures. The Mississippi River Delta was inhabited by Chitimacha and Houma tribes who utilized the region for fishing, hunting, and gathering. Colonial and Antebellum Period

1541 - 1869
▼
European exploration began with Hernando de Soto's 1541 expedition. French colonization in the 1690s established Plaquemines as a strategic buffer, with Fort St. Philip constructed in 1746 for river defense. Spanish rule brought Isleño settlers from the Canary Islands in the 1760s, establishing fishing and trapping communities. The 1803 Louisiana Purchase integrated the area into U.S. territory. The site remained undeveloped wetland throughout the antebellum period.

Post-Civil War to Early Industrialization

1870 - 1932
▼
Post-Reconstruction Plaquemines Parish shifted to commercial fishing, oyster harvesting, and citrus farming. The property remained undeveloped marsh until sulfur deposits were identified in the early 1900s. The Frasch Process (patented 1894) enabled sulfur extraction from salt domes using superheated water. Geological surveys identified sulfur deposits at Lake Grande Écaille. In 1912, Freeport Sulphur Company was founded, establishing the city of Freeport, Texas near its sulfur mines.

Facility Construction and Initial Operations

1932 - 1939
▼
In 1932, Freeport Sulphur Company acquired sulfur mining rights for Lake Grande Écaille (10 miles west of Port Sulphur). Facility construction began in 1932-1933 to support logistics, refining, and shipping operations. The 24' x 433' concrete dock was completed in 1933, rated for 10,000 tons with 52' draft access. Port Sulphur facility began operations in 1933, processing sulfur from Grande Écaille via the Frasch Process. The company town of Port Sulphur grew around the site with worker housing and supporting infrastructure.

Peak Operational Period

1940 - 1978
▼
From 1933-1978, Grande Écaille served as the primary sulfur mine with Port Sulphur as the logistics and shipping hub. The facility supported multiple Frasch Process sulfur mines throughout Louisiana, including offshore Main Pass Block 299 (50 miles offshore, 300' water depth). Wartime production peaked during WWII, earning an Army-Navy "E" Award in 1944 for defense contributions. Expansions in the 1950s added liquid sulfur shipment capabilities (first barge load shipped in 1955). By the 1960s-1970s, the facility operated at peak capacity with extensive "vatting" operations creating acres of dry sulfur storage blocks.

Economic Decline and Closure

1979 - 2005
▼
Primary sulfur mining operations ceased at Grande Écaille mine in 1978 after 45 years. Economic decline began in the 1980s due to market competition from recovered sulfur (oil/gas refining byproducts). In 1981, McMoRan Oil & Gas merged with Freeport Minerals to create Freeport-McMoRan Inc. (FTX). Reduced operations continued through the 1990s at diminished capacity. By the early 2000s, all sulfur operations ceased. Environmental concerns emerged including acid generation, heavy metals mobilization, and groundwater impacts from 72 years of operations. Hurricane Katrina (August 29, 2005) delivered catastrophic damage with 22' storm surge and 75 kt winds, destroying remaining infrastructure and marking the definitive end of Freeport operations.

Post-Katrina Remediation and Current Status

2005 - Present
▼
Post-Katrina site clearance operations began with FEMA assistance, including $1.5M funding for water treatment infrastructure recovery in 2007. In 2007, FCX acquired Phelps Dodge, becoming the world's largest publicly traded copper producer. Environmental assessments were conducted in 2009 under Louisiana DEQ's Risk Evaluation/Corrective Action Program (RECAP) addressing sulfuric acid and heavy metals contamination. Freeport-McMoRan conducted comprehensive remediation from 2020-2024 involving lime neutralization, soil amendments, and groundwater treatment. In 2024, remediation was completed and the site received verified "ready for reuse" status from LDEQ. EPA ECHO database confirms no ongoing enforcement actions. As of September 2025, the 106.99-acre industrial waterfront property is listed for $14.7M, emphasizing deep-water access for potential dock expansion.

Financing Structures & Opportunities

Multiple financing pathways exist for port and terminal infrastructure development, combining public and private capital sources. The Port Sulphur Terminal project is eligible for several tax-advantaged and federal credit programs due to its maritime infrastructure classification.

Municipal Revenue Bonds

Tax-exempt bonds for docks/wharves under IRC §142 (exempt-facility bonds). Repayment from facility rents, fees, or system net revenues.

  • Eligible Use: Docks, wharves, mass commuting facilities
  • Tax Treatment: Tax-exempt interest for investors
  • Repayment Source: Project revenues or port authority system revenues
  • Example: Port Authority consolidated bonds backed by system revenues

Private Activity Bonds (PABs)

Tax-exempt bonds issued by public authority for private projects meeting IRC §142 requirements.

  • Eligible Facilities: Docks, wharves, transportation infrastructure
  • Volume Cap: State allocation required
  • Tax Benefit: Tax-exempt financing for private development
  • Structure: Public issuance with private project repayment obligation

Lease-Revenue Bonds

Bonds repaid from pledged lease revenues under long-term operating agreements.

  • Security: Dedicated lease payment stream
  • Example: Fernandina OHPA bond refinancing (2018)
  • Advantage: Matches debt service to operational cash flows

Public-Private Partnership (P3)

Availability-payment or revenue-risk concessions with long-term private operation.

  • Models: DBFOM (Design-Build-Finance-Operate-Maintain)
  • Example: Port of Miami Tunnel (30-year availability payments)
  • Risk Allocation: Construction risk to private partner, revenue risk varies

TIFIA Loans

Federal credit assistance for transportation infrastructure (Transportation Infrastructure Finance and Innovation Act).

  • Terms: Long-term, low-interest federal loans
  • Example: Gerald Desmond Bridge (Long Beach)
  • Advantage: Below-market rates, flexible repayment

RAISE Grants

Federal grant program for transportation projects (formerly TIGER/BUILD grants).

  • Funding Level: Up to 85% federal funding possible
  • Example: Plaquemines rail spur extension ($72M federal of $85M total)
  • Competitive: Nationwide competition, strong regional benefits required
