The SCM Inflection Point

The US cementitious materials landscape is at a structural inflection point. Coal plant retirements are permanently reducing fly ash supply — 14.5 GW retiring in 2025 alone. Blast furnace closures are constraining slag availability as EAF steelmaking reaches 70%+ market share. Natural pozzolans and LC3 technology are emerging as cost-competitive alternatives, with natural pozzolan pricing (~$115/Mt) converging with fly ash ($118/Mt) for the first time. Tariff actions on cement imports from Turkey, Vietnam, and Canada are simultaneously reshaping $2.4B in annual trade flows. The companies and regions that adapt fastest to this supply chain realignment will gain significant competitive advantage through 2030.

Cement: Tariffs Reshaping Trade

Section 232 tariffs on Canada (25%) and Mexico (25%), plus AD/CVD duties on Vietnam (46%), are restructuring $2.4B in annual cement imports. Turkey holds 36% market share at 10% baseline. The 20M+ MT import gap cannot be closed by domestic production alone — only 72% capacity utilization at 99 plants.

Slag: Structural Supply Crisis

EAF steelmaking now at 70%+ of US production, with only ~7 integrated blast furnaces remaining. Slag cement shipments fell 16% to 3.7 Mt in 2024. Dearborn Works idled March 2025. Import dependency on Japan (52% share) is growing, but Japan is also reducing BF capacity. GGBFS is <3% of slag tonnage but ~80% of value.

Fly Ash: Declining Resource

Coal capacity has dropped from 318 GW peak (2011) to 165 GW, with 68.8 GW of additional retirements scheduled through 2030. CCP production fell to 67M tons in 2023. Reclaimed ash (4.5M tons in 2023) is growing but cannot fully offset fresh supply losses. US prices at $123/MT vs. $16-27/MT Asian FOB create import arbitrage.

Structural Crisis: EAF Transition Eliminates Slag Supply

Electric Arc Furnace (EAF) steelmaking now accounts for 70%+ of US production, up from ~50% in 2005. EAFs produce no granulated blast furnace slag — the feedstock for GGBFS cement. Only ~7 integrated blast furnace mills remain operational. Cleveland-Cliffs and U.S. Steel/Nippon Steel are the last major BF-BOF operators. 16.1 Mt/yr of new EAF capacity is being added (Q4 2023–2025), further accelerating the structural shift. Each blast furnace closure permanently removes slag supply with no substitute. Dearborn Works was idled in March 2025.

GGBFS Import Sources

Import Dependency Growing

As domestic blast furnace capacity declines, GGBFS imports have grown to ~2.0+ Mt/yr valued at $226M (+53% YoY in value). Japan’s share grew from 40% (2019) to 52% (2023), with premium-quality slag from modern integrated mills. Transpacific shipping adds $25–35/MT.

Key risk: Japan is reducing BF capacity under its Green Transformation plan, potentially constraining the largest US import source by 2028. New US terminals (Heidelberg Houston ~500K t/yr, Titan Norfolk $37M upgrade) are expanding import capacity.

2025 Retirement Surge: 14.5 GW Scheduled for Closure

The largest single-year wave of coal retirements hits in 2025 — a +300% increase over 2024. Regional breakdown: MISO 39% (5.7 GW), PJM 27% (3.9 GW), SPP 15% (2.2 GW), Other 19% (2.7 GW). Total scheduled retirements 2025–2030: 68.8 GW. Each closure permanently eliminates a fly ash source. CCP production projected to fall to ~45M tons by 2030.

Price Convergence: Natural Pozzolans Now Cost-Competitive

For the first time, natural pozzolan pricing (~$115/Mt delivered) has converged with fly ash ($118–$123/Mt). Fly ash has risen from $40–60/Mt historically to $118+, while natural pozzolans have remained stable with only modest increases (+15–28% since 2020). Combined with abundant domestic reserves (>25 Mt identified pumice alone, plus Kirkland Mine’s 39 Mt reserves), stable supply chains independent of coal/steel, and embodied CO₂ of just 20–50 kg/t (95–97% lower than OPC), natural pozzolans are positioned for accelerated adoption.

Supply Chain & Logistics

3 Modes National Coverage

Port Infrastructure

24 major import ports handle cement, slag, and SCM imports. Deep-draft facilities (35–45 ft) required for Handysize/Supramax vessels. Gulf Coast (Houston, Tampa, New Orleans) handles 55%+ of volume. Vessel types: Handysize 28–38K DWT, Supramax 48–58K DWT. Discharge rate: ~8,000–10,000 t/day.

Rail Networks

97 of 99 US cement plants are rail-served. Class I carriers (BNSF, UP, CSX, NS) provide long-haul capacity. Covered hopper cars (3,000–4,000 cu ft) transport 500–1,500 miles economically. Average rate: $0.03–0.05/MT-mile. Unit trains: ~100 MT per 100-car consist.

Trucking

150–250 mile economic radius. Pneumatic tanker trailers carry 24–26 MT per load. Last-mile to batch plants and job sites. Rate: $0.15–0.25/MT-mile (3–5x rail cost per mile). Driver shortage constraining capacity in many markets.
