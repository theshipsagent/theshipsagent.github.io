New Orleans Rail Gateway Analysis

Class I Railroad Routes, Switching Operations & Competitive Landscape · Jefferson & Orleans Parishes, Louisiana

Gateway Overview

New Orleans is the fourth-largest rail gateway in the United States by tonnage, serving as a critical interchange point where eastern and western Class I railroads meet at the Mississippi River. The gateway is defined by the New Orleans Rail Gateway (NORG) — a shared 29-mile corridor running from Bridge City on the West Bank through downtown New Orleans to New Orleans East — and is anchored by two specialized switching and terminal entities: the New Orleans Public Belt Railroad (NOPB), now rebranded as Rail NOLA, which provides neutral switching between all six Class I carriers operating in the region.

All six major Class I railroads serving North America have a physical presence at the New Orleans gateway: Union Pacific (UP), BNSF Railway (BNSF), Norfolk Southern (NS), CSX Transportation (CSX), Canadian National (CN), and Canadian Pacific Kansas City (CPKC). This concentration of carriers makes New Orleans one of only a handful of true multi-railroad interchange hubs in the country, and the only one where all six Class I railroads converge at a common neutral switching facility.

NOPB / Rail NOLA — Neutral Switching

The New Orleans Public Belt Railroad (NOPB), now operating as Rail NOLA, was established in 1908 as a publicly owned terminal railroad to provide neutral, non-discriminatory switching between all carriers at the Port of New Orleans. Unlike carrier-owned terminal facilities in other cities, Rail NOLA operates as a neutral party, meaning all six Class I railroads can interchange cars with each other through Rail NOLA's yard system at standardized switching rates, regardless of competitive relationships.

In 2022, the Port of New Orleans assumed full operational control of NOPB and rebranded the organization as Rail NOLA, reflecting an updated strategic focus on integrating rail service with Port NOLA's cargo handling facilities. This governance change was intended to streamline capital investment decisions and align rail operations with port development priorities, including the Louisiana International Terminal (LIT) project.

Critical Bottleneck: Huey P. Long Bridge

The Huey P. Long Bridge rail crossing over the Mississippi River is a single-track structure built in 1935, representing the sole rail connection between the East Bank and West Bank operations of Rail NOLA and the Class I railroads serving both sides of the river. The bridge carries all rail traffic between the eastern carriers (NS, CSX, CN) and the western carriers (UP, BNSF, CPKC), meaning any traffic requiring east-west interline movement — including bulk commodities, grain, chemicals, and containers — must traverse this single-track bottleneck.

The bridge's 20 mph speed restriction, combined with its single-track configuration, creates significant dwell time for cars awaiting bridge crossing windows. Studies have identified the Huey P. Long Bridge as the single greatest capacity constraint in the NORG corridor. A second track across the Mississippi River has been studied for decades and is included in current infrastructure planning discussions, though no funding has been committed for this project as of February 2026.

Gateway Capacity Issues

Operational Constraints — NORG Corridor

The NORG corridor operates at or near capacity under current infrastructure conditions. The combination of a 20 mph blanket speed restriction, a single-track river crossing, 20 at-grade highway crossings, and a constrained freight lead at NS Oliver Yard creates compounding delays that cascade through the system. USDOT forecasts project that Gulf Coast rail tonnage will double by 2030, making current capacity inadequate even without the proposed Louisiana International Terminal adding new demand.

UP-NS Merger Impact

Merger Status — February 2026

Union Pacific announced its proposed acquisition of Norfolk Southern on December 16, 2025, valuing NS at approximately $85 billion. The STB (Surface Transportation Board) received the initial application in late December 2025 but issued a notice on January 16, 2026 that the filing was incomplete and required substantial supplemental information before the formal review clock would begin. A reverse termination fee of $2.5 billion has been disclosed. This would be the first merger application reviewed under the STB's 2001 rules requiring that the transaction "enhance" competition rather than merely not harm it — a significantly higher standard than previous merger reviews.

St. Bernard Corridor Captive Shipper Risk

The St. Bernard Corridor in eastern New Orleans is served exclusively by Norfolk Southern's Oliver Yard. Following the 1996 UP-SP merger, eastern New Orleans shippers retained competition between NS and CSX; however, the geography of the St. Bernard Corridor makes CSX access difficult without NOPB switching, adding cost and dwell time. If UP acquires NS, the following facilities would face significantly reduced competitive options:

  • NS Oliver Yard (primary St. Bernard classification facility)
  • Louisiana International Terminal (being built adjacent to NS-served corridor)
  • Industrial shippers along the St. Bernard / Chef Menteur corridor
  • Chemical plants in the eastern Orleans Parish industrial district
  • Bulk liquid terminals relying on NS unit train service to eastern US refineries
  • Import cement terminals receiving eastern US origination via NS intermodal

Post-Merger Captive Position

Shippers currently served solely or primarily by NS at the St. Bernard Corridor would, post-merger, face a UP-NS monopoly for direct rail service. While the STB's Committed Gateway Pricing (CGP) mechanism provides some rate protection at interchange gateways, CGP has significant gaps: it does not apply to single-line traffic, it is subject to shipper application requirements, and it excludes certain commodity categories. The practical effect is that shippers who today can play NS against CSX for competitive rate quotes would lose that leverage, and CGP would not fully substitute for true carrier competition.
