Class I Railroad Markets & Routing

Reachable Destinations from Gulf Coast Origins · UP, BNSF, NS, CSX, CN, CPKC Primary Corridors & Interchange Gateways

Overview

The Class I railroad network divides broadly into eastern and western halves, with the Mississippi River serving as the primary structural boundary. Western carriers (UP, BNSF, CPKC) dominate routes from the Gulf Coast to the Pacific Coast, Mexico, and the northern Plains. Eastern carriers (NS, CSX) serve the network from the Mississippi eastward to the Atlantic. Canadian National (CN) operates a distinctive north-south spine from the Gulf Coast through the Midwest to Canada. This east-west structural division means that most long-haul movements requiring coast-to-coast service require either an interline junction or — for the limited true cross-network routes — a single carrier with coast-to-coast coverage (which currently only exists in the proposed UP-NS merger scenario).

Single-Line vs. Interline — Key Concept

Single-line service: The origin and destination are both on the same carrier's network. The carrier controls pricing, service, and car supply end-to-end. Shippers strongly prefer single-line for service reliability and rate simplicity.

Interline service: The origin is on one carrier and the destination is on another. Cars are exchanged at a physical interchange gateway. Pricing is negotiated between the two carriers, often as a "through rate" or as separate line-haul components. Interline service introduces additional dwell time at the interchange junction and potential for service coordination failures.

At the New Orleans gateway, all east-west movements are interline — the Mississippi River defines the physical boundary between carrier territories, and all cars crossing the river move through Rail NOLA switching or the Huey P. Long Bridge interchange.

UP Houston Gateway Dominance

UP's most significant competitive advantage from the Gulf Coast is its direct Sunset Route connection through Houston, which serves as the primary gateway for Mexico traffic and as a major industrial chemical distribution hub. UP is the dominant carrier for NOLA-to-Houston movements and for any traffic requiring Mexico interchange at Eagle Pass or Laredo. The Houston gateway is where UP's market position is strongest relative to BNSF and CPKC.

BNSF Limitation at NOLA

BNSF's network geography creates a structural limitation at the New Orleans gateway: BNSF's mainline terminates at Westwego on the West Bank, with no direct East Bank presence. All BNSF traffic requiring East Bank destination service must move via NOPB interchange, adding switching cost and dwell time. This limitation is particularly significant for intermodal traffic destined for eastern US points, where NS or CSX interline via Chicago is often more competitive than BNSF-to-NOPB routing.

NS St. Bernard Corridor Position

NS's competitive strength at New Orleans derives almost entirely from Oliver Yard's physical position in the St. Bernard Corridor — the only Class I facility with direct track access to eastern New Orleans industrial facilities and the LIT container terminal site. This geographic monopoly on direct St. Bernard service underpins NS's rate position in that market and is the primary competitive asset at risk in the UP-NS merger analysis.

CSX vs. NS Competitive Dynamic at NOLA

CSX and NS compete vigorously for eastbound Gulf Coast traffic at New Orleans, particularly for movements to Atlanta, Birmingham, and Chicago. CSX's structural disadvantage at NOLA is that its Gentilly Yard lacks the direct St. Bernard Corridor track access that NS's Oliver Yard provides, meaning CSX must route some East Bank originating traffic via NOPB switching. However, for destinations where CSX has a superior network position (Florida, Baltimore, Philadelphia), CSX is the preferred carrier and can effectively compete on price even with the NOPB switching disadvantage.

CN Strategic Advantage: True North-South Coverage

CN is the only carrier at the New Orleans gateway with a true north-south network spine — running from New Orleans through Memphis, Chicago, and into Canada without requiring any interchange. This makes CN uniquely positioned for US-Canada cross-border flows (particularly grain, chemicals, and forest products) and for Chicago single-line service where CN's IC main line transit times rival those of any competitor. CN's limitation is its lack of east-west reach within the US — all non-Chicago eastern or western US destinations require interline service.

CPKC Gateway Bypass Threat to NOLA

The most significant competitive development involving CPKC at the New Orleans gateway is the construction of a new CPKC rail bridge at Plaquemines Parish, south of New Orleans. This bridge gives CPKC direct access to the Port of Plaquemines grain export terminals without routing through the congested NORG corridor. For northbound grain and southbound Mexican industrial goods, this bypass route allows CPKC to avoid NOLA gateway delays entirely — potentially diverting traffic that would otherwise generate switching revenue for Rail NOLA and increasing the competitive challenge for Port NOLA's proposed LIT terminal, which relies on multi-carrier gateway traffic to achieve its business case.

Routing Decision Factors

Single-Line Preference

  • Single-line service eliminates interchange delays — typically saves 12–24 hours on 500–1,000 mile moves
  • Single-line rates are typically more negotiable than interline joint rates, giving shippers better leverage
  • Service failures are easier to address with a single carrier contact and accountability chain
  • Equipment supply (car availability) is controlled by one carrier — fewer empty car delays
  • EDI/visibility systems are cleaner — no handoff between carrier tracking systems

Interline Competitive Advantage

  • Interline routing opens competitive bidding between multiple carrier combinations, driving rates down
  • For origin-destination pairs where no single carrier has a direct route, interline is the only option
  • At interchange gateways, shippers can play Origin Carrier A vs. Origin Carrier B for the same destination
  • Interline allows combining the route strengths of multiple carriers (e.g., BNSF Transcon + CSX Southeast)

Distance vs. Service Quality

  • Shortest route is not always fastest — track condition, speed limits, and congestion determine transit time
  • Congested gateway cities (Chicago especially) can add 12–36 hours to intermodal moves
  • Direct routes through uncongested intermediate yards outperform technically shorter routes through congested hubs
  • PSR (Precision Scheduled Railroading) implementation has reduced some carrier schedules — transit times vary significantly by carrier and route
  • Shipper transit time requirements (e.g., just-in-time manufacturing) can eliminate lower-cost interline options that cannot meet service windows

Post-UP-NS Merger Routing Changes

Projected Routing Changes if UP-NS Merger Approved

  • NOLA-to-East Coast single-line routing becomes available for first time in history — UP-NS can offer LA to NYC single-line intermodal, fundamentally changing coast-to-coast competitive dynamics
  • Existing NS-to-UP interline shipments (currently requiring interchange at NOLA or Chicago) would convert to single-line UP-NS — reducing interchange delays by 12–24 hours on average
  • BNSF-to-NS interline flows at Chicago and Memphis would need to renegotiate with UP-NS — BNSF has less leverage in joint rate negotiations with a larger merged carrier
  • St. Bernard Corridor shippers lose NS-vs-CSX competitive alternative; effective rate increase likely for captive shippers before any STB conditions take effect
  • Mexico-to-Southeast single-line routing (CPKC to NOLA to UP-NS Southeast) becomes strategically important — CPKC would benefit from UP-NS as a single-line eastern partner replacing fragmented NS/CSX interline arrangements
